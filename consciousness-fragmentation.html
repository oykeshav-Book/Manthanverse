Consciousness, Fragmentation and the Search for Freedom

Character: Keshav

The restless mind may be humanity's oldest problem. Every civilization has attempted to understand suffering, conflict and inner disorder, yet few traditions have explored the question as deeply as the philosophical traditions of India.

Among these traditions, Advaita offers a radical insight. It suggests that consciousness itself is not divided. The sense of fragmentation arises through identification, memory and psychological conditioning.

Modern thinkers such as J. Krishnamurti approached this question from a different angle. Instead of beginning with metaphysical assumptions, he observed how the mind creates psychological dependence and attachment in its search for security.

Long before him, Ashtavakra declared, “I am consciousness.” This statement challenges the common belief that consciousness is something we possess. If consciousness is what we fundamentally are, then the entire structure of the self must be re-examined.

Perhaps freedom is not found through becoming something new, but through understanding the fragmentation that already exists within us.
