Who Is Reborn?

A conversation between Advait and Nakul exploring one of humanity's oldest questions: Who is actually reborn? The body, the mind, the soul, or something else entirely?

Characters: Advait • Nakul

Nakul

Hey Advait, do you really believe in reincarnation?

Advait

Yes, I do. Why don't you? By the way, tell me something first—do you think reincarnation happens, or not?

Nakul

I don't believe in it because I haven't seen any scientific evidence for it.

But first, tell me what you mean by reincarnation.

Do you mean that the same soul leaves one body and enters another?

Advait

Do you think the soul is some kind of prisoner trapped inside a body, and after death it simply enters another body?

Nakul

No. That's just the common definition people usually give.

So tell me, what is the soul according to you, and how does reincarnation actually work?

Advait

First, you need to understand something.

The soul, consciousness, or awareness is not something divided into separate pieces.

Suppose I draw a circle. You think the circle is the body and the dot inside it is the soul.

But the actual picture is the opposite.

Imagine a blank page. That blank page is consciousness.

The circle is drawn on that page—that is the body.

Inside that circle are smaller circles, and inside them even smaller circles. Those are just different levels within consciousness.

Do you understand?

Nakul

I think so.

You're saying consciousness isn't a small entity sitting inside the body.

The body exists within consciousness, not the other way around.

Then here's my question:

If consciousness is one and everything exists within it, then what exactly is being "reborn" in reincarnation?

Who is born again?

Advait

Before answering that, let me ask you something.

Science says energy can neither be created nor destroyed—it can only be transformed.

Doesn't science say something similar about information?

Nakul

Energy, yes.

Information is a little more complicated.

In modern physics, especially quantum physics, many scientists believe information may not actually be destroyed, although the subject is still debated.

But why are you asking this? What does it have to do with reincarnation?

Advait

What do you think your identity really is?

Your body?

Or the information that makes you who you are?

If we talk about reincarnation, what exactly should be reborn?

Nakul

From a scientific point of view, my identity isn't just my body.

It's the combination of my memories, experiences, brain structure, personality, and information patterns.

So if reincarnation makes any sense, it would make more sense for information to continue than for the body to continue.

But then comes the real question:

How is that information preserved after death?

Advait

Hinduism often speaks of subtle bodies within the physical body.

Reincarnation belongs to those subtle bodies.

They're not physical.

They're formed by one's karma.

Think of the brain.

Science says the brain doesn't store experiences like a video recording.

It stores raw information, and our mind reconstructs experience from it.

Nakul

So you're saying the subtle body is something like an information structure created by karma, tendencies and impressions?

And you're comparing that to the way the brain stores information?

But then I have a question.

Science always needs a medium to carry information.

Neurons carry brain information.

DNA carries genetic information.

What carries the subtle body if it isn't physical?

Advait

You remember what I asked you in the beginning?

You said that, according to modern physics, information may not actually be destroyed.

That's exactly my point.

This information is part of the universe itself.

What we call identity isn't the body.

It's the accumulated pattern of karma, tendencies, desires, impressions, and experiences.

The body is only the medium through which that pattern is expressed.

When the body dies, the information doesn't suddenly become nothing.

It continues.

And wherever the conditions are suitable, it expresses itself again.

That's why Hinduism says,

"As your karma, so your next birth."

Not because someone is sitting somewhere judging you.

But because information naturally unfolds according to its own nature.

Nakul

I think I'm beginning to understand your model.

You're not saying a tiny soul leaves one body and enters another.

You're saying identity itself is an evolving information pattern.

But I still have a question.

If that's true, will the next person remember being Advait?

Advait

How could they?

Advait isn't the origin of that information.

Advait only modifies it according to his own capacity.

Think of a piggy bank.

For years, coins are constantly added and removed.

At the end, you see whatever coins remain.

Can you tell exactly where each one originally came from?

Or how many times they were exchanged before ending up there?

You can't.

Identity works the same way.

You can't trace every tendency back to a single birth.

You don't know where the information originally came from.

You don't know what was transformed.

You don't know what remained.

All you know is the pattern that exists now.

Nakul

So you're saying the real question isn't,

"Who was I in my previous life?"

Because there may not even be a meaningful answer to that.

The current pattern may contain influences accumulated over countless lives.

Advait

Exactly.

That's why I think people misunderstand reincarnation.

Mostly because of movies.

They imagine that after death the same person wakes up in another body with the same memories waiting to be recovered.

But that's not how I understand it.

In fact, even in Hindu traditions, many great sages never claimed to remember their previous births.

Past-life memory was never presented as the foundation of reincarnation.

Nakul

That's actually true.

I think I was rejecting the popular version of reincarnation rather than the philosophical one.

If identity is a continuously changing information stream, then expecting complete autobiographical memories doesn't even make much sense.

The body is different.

The brain is different.

The experiences are different.

Why should the memories remain exactly the same?

Advait

Exactly.

What may continue isn't the story.

It's the tendencies.

Suppose, for no obvious reason, I deeply love mountains or rainfall.

That doesn't necessarily mean I'll remember living in the mountains in some previous life.

It simply means that after countless transformations, only that tendency remained.

The rest of the information was modified, merged, or transformed over time.

The entire backstory disappeared.

Only the tendency survived.

Nakul

Hold on...

A minute ago I was beginning to agree with you.

Where did my scientific mind go? (laughs)

Advait

It should stay exactly where it is.

I'm not asking you to abandon science.

Nakul

Then let me ask the questions a scientist would ask.

Your model is internally consistent.

But consistency alone isn't evidence.

Where is this information stored after death?

What carries it?

How does it connect to a new body?

And most importantly...

How could we ever test it?

Without evidence, science can only treat it as a hypothesis.

Advait

I never claimed I was proving anything.

The evidence, if it exists, will have to be discovered by science itself.

Science has always moved forward by asking questions first and finding evidence later.

To understand what carries this information, we first have to understand consciousness itself.

Let me give you an example.

Imagine you're playing an open-world RPG.

You can choose any character—a bird, an animal, a man, a woman, or anything else.

Which one would you choose?

Nakul

Probably a human.

Maybe someone intelligent and curious.

Or perhaps an eagle, just to experience flying.

But why are you asking?

Advait

Think about it carefully.

You never actually enter that character.

The game already exists.

Its rules are already fixed.

You simply choose a role based on your curiosity and experience the game through it.

The character isn't you.

It's just the interface through which you experience that world.

Nakul

So in your analogy...

The player is consciousness.

The character is the body.

The game's rules are karma or nature.

And the storyline is the present life.

Interesting.

But here's my scientific objection.

In a game, we know the player exists outside the character.

How do you know consciousness exists independently of the body in real life?

An analogy isn't evidence.

Advait

I already told you.

I can explain the idea.

I can't produce scientific evidence.

That is science's responsibility, not mine.

Just as the player exists beyond the character in a game, consciousness exists beyond the body.

I'm explaining a possibility.

Science has to investigate whether it's true.

Nakul

Fair enough.

Then I wouldn't reject your model.

But I wouldn't accept it either.

I'd simply say:

"It's an interesting philosophical framework that still awaits evidence."

Advait

I'm not even proposing a new theory.

I'm asking a simple question.

Where do your desires go after death?

Where does attachment go?

Where does love go?

Where does fear go?

Sound waves don't simply vanish.

Light doesn't simply vanish.

Science itself tells us that DNA carries information.

Information is transmitted.

So why do we immediately assume that the information which shapes identity simply disappears?

Nakul

That's a fair question.

Science certainly accepts that information exists and can be transmitted.

DNA is one example.

But when it comes to personality, desires, and consciousness...

Science honestly doesn't have a complete answer yet.

So I can't tell you where that information goes after death.

At the same time, I also can't simply assume that it survives.

That's where the question remains open.
