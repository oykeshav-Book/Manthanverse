What Do We Really Mean by Love?

A conversation between Keshav and Gopi exploring the nature of love, attachment, devotion, and the journey from ordinary affection to divine love.

Characters: Keshav • Gopi

Keshav: Gopi, have you ever been in love? 🙂

Gopi: Ohho... you're really eager to know, aren't you? Do you even know what love is?

Keshav: No, that's exactly why I'm asking. People talk so much about love, but everyone seems to have a different definition of it. What's yours? 😄

Gopi: Love cannot really be defined. That's precisely why it is love.

Keshav: Really? Then how do people recognize that what they're feeling is love and not something else?

Gopi: Why do you think you're in love in the first place?

Keshav: Maybe because the heart has a habit of giving a name to everything that feels deep. 😄

But alright, let me ask the opposite question—if someone doesn't even realize that they're in love, can love still be present?

Gopi: They may call it something else, but if love is there, it cannot go unnoticed.

Keshav: So according to you, love cannot remain hidden? Meaning that the day it truly happens, whether a person accepts it or not, they will definitely know?

Gopi: Yes. Because love is experiential in nature. If there is no experience of it, then what kind of love is that?

Keshav: Yes, that sounds reasonable. If there is no experience at all, then it would remain nothing more than an imagination or an idea.

But then tell me, what does the experience of love actually feel like?

Gopi: It is difficult to confine love within words. But saints have described some of its characteristics—it is connected with truth, consciousness, and bliss; it grows with every passing moment; it is beyond selfish desire; it is extremely subtle and can only be directly experienced.

What the experience of love feels like depends on its stage and depth. But its movement is always in the direction of love itself.

Understand?

Keshav: I understand... or at least I'm trying to. 😄

You're saying that love is not an ordinary emotion. It contains awareness, bliss, and it keeps growing every moment.

But then I have a confusion...

If someone has desire, selfishness, and love all at the same time, is that love, or is it merely a movement toward love?

Gopi: First, one must see what the desire is for, toward whom it is directed, and what motivates it. Not every desire is the same.

If the desire is driven by one's own pleasure, possession, or the urge to obtain something, then it may be closer to attachment or rati.

And if the desire is for the well-being and happiness of the beloved, then it may be moving in the direction of love.

In its fullness, love is beyond desire and selfishness. Therefore, based on this alone, one cannot definitively say whether it is love or not.

However, if confusion, selfishness, and possessiveness are very strong, then it is possible that love has not yet fully manifested, and the person is still in some intermediate state between rati, attachment, and love.

Keshav: Hmm... so you're saying that merely knowing "there is desire" is not enough. We need to see what the desire is actually for.

If I want someone because they give me happiness, then perhaps that is really about my own happiness.

And if I wish for their well-being, whether I gain anything from it or not, then that's something different.

But tell me one thing, Gopi... if someone says, "I can't live without you," would that seem like love to you, or attachment?

Gopi: That cannot be determined from words alone. Love must be experienced directly.

And we would also need to see what kind of experience arises when one is with that person.

But from words alone, nothing definite can be said.

Keshav: Then it seems I've been looking for answers in the wrong place. 😄

I've been trying to grasp love through words, and you keep directing me toward experience.

But there's still a difficulty...

How can someone who has never been in love know that the experience you're talking about is actually love?

Gopi: First of all, we need to distinguish between the kind of love you're talking about and other forms of love.

Some forms of love are present in almost everyone's life.

Don't you feel affection for your friends, parents, teacher, or God?

And you can recognize that each of those relationships carries a different feeling and mood, can't you?

So you also know that you love them.

Keshav: Yes, there is affection... and it feels different in each case.

One kind with my mother, another with friends, and perhaps something different altogether with God.

So you're saying that love is not some rare thing that suddenly falls from the sky one day. It is already present in life; it simply appears in different forms?

Then why do people so often say, "I've fallen in love for the first time"? 😄

Gopi: No, that's not what I said.

What I'm saying is that the love you're referring to is probably mādhurya-prema (romantic love).

In reality, love has many forms or rasas—such as mādhurya (romantic), vātsalya (parental), sakhya (friendship), dāsya (servitude), and śānta (peaceful reverence).

And all of them begin with rati.

Keshav: I see... so I was taking the word love in only one sense, while you're viewing it in many forms.

That means a mother's love and a beloved's love are both love, but their rasa is different.

And you said something else—that they all begin with rati.

So is rati like a seed and prema like a tree?

Or am I oversimplifying things again? 😄

Gopi: Yes, you're understanding it somewhat correctly.

Look, eight stages of love have been described:

Rati, Prema, Sneha, Maan, Pranaya, Raaga, Anuraaga, and Mahabhava.

Keshav: Wow... until now I thought love was a single step, and you've brought an entire staircase. 😄

So what ordinary people often call love might actually still be only the first step.

But tell me, what is the first clear distinction between rati and prema?

Gopi: In rati, the feeling has just begun to sprout. A sense of attachment or attraction is felt.

But that same rati can also move toward lust, delusion, attachment, or greed.

If its direction remains pure, then it progresses toward prema.

In prema, a person regards the beloved as equal to oneself and considers the welfare of both.

Keshav: Hmm... so rati is a possibility, not the destination.

Attraction and attachment have appeared, but the direction has not yet been decided.

It can move downward or upward.

And upon reaching prema, the distinction between "me" and "you" becomes lighter, and the well-being of both becomes important.

Tell me one thing, Gopi...

If two people genuinely wish for each other's welfare, but they do not have that deep experience, that ever-growing bliss, would you call that love or simply goodness?

Gopi: It could be humanity, friendship, a sense of duty, or some combination of these.

One cannot determine love or the absence of love from such a small amount of information.

That is exactly why I keep saying that love cannot be fully captured through a few characteristics.

Characteristics can help, but certainty about love ultimately comes only through experience.

Yes, saints have described some indications of it—being connected with truth, consciousness, and bliss; ever-increasing; beyond selfish desire; extremely subtle; and experiential in nature.

But knowing these characteristics and knowing love are not the same thing.

Keshav: So we're back to the same point...

I'm asking for a map, and you keep sending me on the journey itself. 😄

Gopi: Perhaps because some places cannot be understood by looking at a map—they can only be understood by walking the path yourself.
