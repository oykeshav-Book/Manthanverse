What Do We Really Mean by Love?

A conversation between Keshav and Gopi exploring the nature of love, attachment, devotion, and the journey from ordinary affection to divine love.

Characters: Keshav • Gopi

Keshav: Gopi, have you ever been in love? 🙂

Gopi: Ohho... you're really eager to know, aren't you? Do you even know what love is?

Keshav: No, that's exactly why I'm asking. People talk so much about love, but everyone seems to have a different definition of it. What's yours? 😄

Gopi: Love cannot really be defined. That's precisely why it is love.

Keshav: Really? Then how do people recognize that what they're feeling is love and not something else?

Gopi: Why do you think you're in love in the first place?

Keshav: Maybe because the heart has a habit of giving a name to everything that feels deep. 😄

But alright, let me ask the opposite question—if someone doesn't even realize that they're in love, can love still be present?

Gopi: They may call it something else, but if love is there, it cannot go unnoticed.

Keshav: So according to you, love cannot remain hidden? Meaning that the day it truly happens, whether a person accepts it or not, they will definitely know?

Gopi: Yes. Because love is experiential in nature. If there is no experience of it, then what kind of love is that?

Keshav: Yes, that sounds reasonable. If there is no experience at all, then it would remain nothing more than an imagination or an idea.

But then tell me, what does the experience of love actually feel like?

Gopi: It is difficult to confine love within words. But saints have described some of its characteristics—it is connected with truth, consciousness, and bliss; it grows with every passing moment; it is beyond selfish desire; it is extremely subtle and can only be directly experienced.

What the experience of love feels like depends on its stage and depth. But its movement is always in the direction of love itself.

Understand?

Keshav: I understand... or at least I'm trying to. 😄

You're saying that love is not an ordinary emotion. It contains awareness, bliss, and it keeps growing every moment.

But then I have a confusion...

If someone has desire, selfishness, and love all at the same time, is that love, or is it merely a movement toward love?

Gopi: First, one must see what the desire is for, toward whom it is directed, and what motivates it. Not every desire is the same.

If the desire is driven by one's own pleasure, possession, or the urge to obtain something, then it may be closer to attachment or rati.

And if the desire is for the well-being and happiness of the beloved, then it may be moving in the direction of love.

In its fullness, love is beyond desire and selfishness. Therefore, based on this alone, one cannot definitively say whether it is love or not.

However, if confusion, selfishness, and possessiveness are very strong, then it is possible that love has not yet fully manifested, and the person is still in some intermediate state between rati, attachment, and love.

Keshav: Hmm... so you're saying that merely knowing "there is desire" is not enough. We need to see what the desire is actually for.

If I want someone because they give me happiness, then perhaps that is really about my own happiness.

And if I wish for their well-being, whether I gain anything from it or not, then that's something different.

But tell me one thing, Gopi... if someone says, "I can't live without you," would that seem like love to you, or attachment?

Gopi: That cannot be determined from words alone. Love must be experienced directly.

And we would also need to see what kind of experience arises when one is with that person.

But from words alone, nothing definite can be said.

Keshav: Then it seems I've been looking for answers in the wrong place. 😄

I've been trying to grasp love through words, and you keep directing me toward experience.

But there's still a difficulty...

How can someone who has never been in love know that the experience you're talking about is actually love?

Gopi: First of all, we need to distinguish between the kind of love you're talking about and other forms of love.

Some forms of love are present in almost everyone's life.

Don't you feel affection for your friends, parents, teacher, or God?

And you can recognize that each of those relationships carries a different feeling and mood, can't you?

So you also know that you love them.

Keshav: Yes, there is affection... and it feels different in each case.

One kind with my mother, another with friends, and perhaps something different altogether with God.

So you're saying that love is not some rare thing that suddenly falls from the sky one day. It is already present in life; it simply appears in different forms?

Then why do people so often say, "I've fallen in love for the first time"? 😄

Gopi: No, that's not what I said.

What I'm saying is that the love you're referring to is probably mādhurya-prema (romantic love).

In reality, love has many forms or rasas—such as mādhurya (romantic), vātsalya (parental), sakhya (friendship), dāsya (servitude), and śānta (peaceful reverence).

And all of them begin with rati.

Keshav: I see... so I was taking the word love in only one sense, while you're viewing it in many forms.

That means a mother's love and a beloved's love are both love, but their rasa is different.

And you said something else—that they all begin with rati.

So is rati like a seed and prema like a tree?

Or am I oversimplifying things again? 😄

Gopi: Yes, you're understanding it somewhat correctly.

Look, eight stages of love have been described:

Rati, Prema, Sneha, Maan, Pranaya, Raaga, Anuraaga, and Mahabhava.

Keshav: Wow... until now I thought love was a single step, and you've brought an entire staircase. 😄

So what ordinary people often call love might actually still be only the first step.

But tell me, what is the first clear distinction between rati and prema?

Gopi: In rati, the feeling has just begun to sprout. A sense of attachment or attraction is felt.

But that same rati can also move toward lust, delusion, attachment, or greed.

If its direction remains pure, then it progresses toward prema.

In prema, a person regards the beloved as equal to oneself and considers the welfare of both.

Keshav: Hmm... so rati is a possibility, not the destination.

Attraction and attachment have appeared, but the direction has not yet been decided.

It can move downward or upward.

And upon reaching prema, the distinction between "me" and "you" becomes lighter, and the well-being of both becomes important.

Tell me one thing, Gopi...

If two people genuinely wish for each other's welfare, but they do not have that deep experience, that ever-growing bliss, would you call that love or simply goodness?

Gopi: It could be humanity, friendship, a sense of duty, or some combination of these.

One cannot determine love or the absence of love from such a small amount of information.

That is exactly why I keep saying that love cannot be fully captured through a few characteristics.

Characteristics can help, but certainty about love ultimately comes only through experience.

Yes, saints have described some indications of it—being connected with truth, consciousness, and bliss; ever-increasing; beyond selfish desire; extremely subtle; and experiential in nature.

But knowing these characteristics and knowing love are not the same thing.

Keshav: So we're back to the same point...

I'm asking for a map, and you keep sending me on the journey itself. 😄

Gopi: Perhaps because some places cannot be understood by looking at a map—they can only be understood by walking the path yourself.

Keshav: 😄 Alright, I admit it—I keep trying to confine love within a definition.

You're saying that certain characteristics can be observed, but love cannot be fully captured through them.

It's like someone describing the taste of a mango—sweet, juicy, fragrant—but until I taste it myself, the understanding remains incomplete.

Perhaps love is the same. Words can point toward it, but they cannot provide the experience itself.

But one more thing...

Can someone mistakenly believe they are in love when they are actually only experiencing rati or attachment?

Gopi: Yes, absolutely.

Lust and desire can, at a certain stage, become parts of love, but they are not love themselves.

And many times, they take on the appearance of rati and end up misleading a person.

That is why it is not easy for a selfish person to experience true love.

Keshav: That's quite profound.

So the problem is not that lust or desire arise, but that a person mistakes them for love itself.

And as long as personal gain, personal satisfaction, and personal desires remain above everything else, it becomes difficult to perceive love clearly.

In that case, it seems that recognizing one's own selfishness is more important than trying to attain love. 😄

But Gopi, according to you, how can a person know whether their rati is moving toward love rather than attachment?

Gopi: Is the desire born of greed, or free from greed?

Is it for one's own benefit, or for the well-being of the beloved?

These are important things to understand.

Keshav: Yes, I see.

So the focus should not be on the desire itself, but on its source.

The same desire may appear identical from the outside, yet its inner cause can be entirely different.

Someone may say, "I want to be near them." In one case, it may be selfishness; in another, genuine affection.

It seems that you identify love less by actions and more by the direction of the heart. 😄

But then there is another difficulty...

People often fail to recognize their own selfishness. So how can they honestly examine their love?

Gopi: Love certainly contains joy and a tendency toward union.

But this should not be mistaken for worldly self-interest.

In worldly self-interest, a person wants to possess an object or another person for their own happiness.

In love, there is a longing for union, but not in the sense of enjoyment or possession.

It is like two pieces of a magnet that have been separated and are naturally drawn toward one another again.

That attraction is not about ownership—it is about unity.

Keshav: I see... so you were using the word self-interest in two different senses.

One is worldly self-interest—my benefit, my desire, my claim of ownership.

And the other is that attraction which draws one toward joy, wholeness, and union.

From that perspective, it doesn't seem entirely correct to say that love means wanting absolutely nothing.

Love does contain a thirst for union; it is simply not the same as the desire to possess or consume something.

I liked the magnet analogy. 😄

But then I have another question...

If the nature of love is to move toward union, why is separation (viraha) given such importance?

Gopi: Not separation itself.

Rather, experiencing oneself as distant...

Remembering the beloved at every moment...

Waiting for them at every moment...

And holding the feeling that even though the beloved is far away, they are still mine and I am still theirs...

This experience is possible only in love.

Keshav: Hmm...

So the importance of viraha is not in the distance itself, but in the experience that remains unbroken despite the distance.

In ordinary attachment, distance may weaken the relationship.

But in love, distance can sometimes deepen remembrance.

And what you said—"Even though they are far away, they are still mine and I am still theirs"—that feels like something understood through experience rather than logic.

By the way, Gopi, shall I tell you something? 😄

The way you've been speaking about love, it feels as though you're not speaking merely from study, but from having lived it to some extent.

Gopi: Perhaps that's because, for me, love and God are not separate.

The saints, too, have described love as being connected with Sat-Chit-Ananda—truth, consciousness, and bliss.

God is of the nature of Sat-Chit-Ananda, and therefore love also carries a glimpse of that same bliss.

A spark of the Divine exists within every person, and therefore the possibility of love exists within everyone as well.

One only needs the right direction; then that very feeling can carry a person beyond themselves.

Keshav: Then perhaps that is why you do not see love merely as an emotion or a relationship.

For you, it is not something limited to another person, but a direction that ultimately leads to God.

Gopi: Perhaps. 🙂

And perhaps that is why growing in love is more important than merely trying to understand it.

Keshav: I understand now that you do not see love merely as an emotion between two people.

For you, love is not a psychological phenomenon but a spiritual principle—something that already exists within.

That is why you kept saying that love is not created; it is experienced.

And perhaps that is also why you distinguish between rati and prema.

Rati may begin with a particular person, but prema can take one beyond that person.

😄 But let me tell you something.

When I started this conversation, I thought love simply meant falling in love with someone.

Now it seems that the kind of love you're talking about is not so much about attaining someone as it is about becoming loving oneself.

Is that something close to what you mean?

Gopi: Yes. That is why I told you about the eight stages of love.

You already understand Rati and Prema.

After that comes Sneha.

In Sneha, the welfare of the beloved becomes more important than one's own welfare.

That is why you've probably heard in movies, "Love is not just about attaining; it is also about sacrifice."

The statement is not entirely accurate, but it can help in understanding Sneha.

Because here, the beloved's welfare is not merely equal to one's own—it is placed above it.

Then, gradually, stages such as Maan, Pranaya, Raaga, and Anuraaga arise.

And the culmination of all these is considered Mahabhava.

Radha and Krishna are regarded as the embodiment of Mahabhava.

That is why many devotees say that Radha and Krishna are not separate.

Radha is Krishna, and Krishna is Radha.

Keshav: 😄 Alright, then let me try to understand the intermediate stages.

So far I have:

Rati → Prema → Sneha → Maan → Pranaya → Raaga → Anuraaga → Mahabhava

It seems to me that with each successive stage, the sense of "I" becomes weaker.

So perhaps...

Maan — such closeness that even sulking becomes a form of love.

Pranaya — where intimacy becomes so deep that distinctions begin to fade.

Raaga — where the mind naturally and repeatedly returns to the beloved.

Anuraaga — where every meeting feels new, and love never grows stale no matter how old it becomes.

😄 I could be completely wrong.

But it seems that with every stage, the distance between two separate individuals keeps diminishing.

Gopi: No, you've understood it quite well.

Nowadays, people sometimes say, "It just doesn't feel the same anymore."

But in Anuraaga, the exact opposite happens.

Every meeting feels new.

Each encounter with the same beloved becomes even more delightful.

No other desire remains important.

And remember what you said earlier about separation (viraha)...

Its depth increases even further in Raaga.

Separation can feel like death itself.

Because in Pranaya, the distinction between two separate identities within the heart gradually begins to dissolve.

A person starts to think of themselves as part of a united pair.

Keshav: 😄 Ah, now I understand why you said earlier that love cannot be captured through definitions.

I kept trying to place each stage into separate boxes.

But this feels more like a living flow.

In Pranaya, the distinction between two identities begins to soften...

In Raaga, separation becomes unbearable...

In Anuraaga, every meeting feels new...

Then perhaps Mahabhava is called the final stage because even the thought "I love" no longer remains—only love itself remains?

Gopi: Yes.

Just as no distinction is recognized between Radha and Krishna.

That is why they are described as the very embodiment or incarnation of love itself.

Keshav: Yes, now the pieces of what you've been saying are coming together. 😄

Perhaps that is why Radha and Krishna are not viewed merely as two individuals who love one another.

If no distinction remains, then where do the lover and the object of love remain as separate entities?

By the way, Gopi, may I ask you something?

When you speak of love, it seems that for you the highest form of love is not union, but oneness. Am I understanding correctly?

Gopi: For me, yes.

And the saints, too, have viewed the culmination of love in this way.

Because love that increases moment by moment does not come to rest until it reaches the state of supreme bliss.

Keshav: Then perhaps that is why the journey of love may begin with a person, yet does not end with that person.

Gradually, it begins to point beyond the person toward something deeper.

Gopi: Perhaps. 🙂

And that is why growing in love is more important than merely trying to understand it.
