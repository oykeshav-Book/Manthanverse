What is Consciousness and Is It One or Many?

A conversation between Keshav and Advait exploring whether consciousness, the soul, and God are ultimately different realities—or different ways of describing the same truth.

Characters: Keshav • Advait

Keshav: Yaar, tell me something.

The soul, consciousness, God, the mind... are these all different things, or are they just different names for the same thing?

You spend all your time reading Advaita. You probably know better than I do.

Advait: Let's keep the mind out of it for a moment.

The mind is an instrument.

The real question is about the other three.

According to Advaita, the Self and Brahman are not different.

But before we go there, we need to understand what we mean by consciousness.

Tell me first.

What do you think consciousness is?

Keshav: I'm not sure if I'm right.

But it feels to me as though consciousness exists in levels.

The Mandukya Upanishad speaks of different states—waking, dreaming, deep sleep.

But I don't think those are the only divisions.

A tiny cell seems conscious in its own way.

Cells form organs.

Organs form bodies.

Bodies form worlds.

Everything experiences consciousness at its own level.

And when consciousness comes to know itself completely...

Perhaps nothing remains apart from it.

Maybe that's what people call God.

Advait: Let me ask you something.

Are there really different levels of consciousness?

Or is consciousness one, while its expression appears different at different levels?

The second possibility makes more sense to me.

Think of sunlight.

The sun doesn't change.

But a small window lets in less light than a large one.

What changes is not the sun.

Only the degree to which it is expressed.

Keshav: No... I don't completely agree.

Or maybe I do.

I also believe the brain doesn't create consciousness.

It only limits it.

Every living being has different limits.

That's why every being experiences reality differently.

But I don't think consciousness itself ever becomes greater or smaller.

What changes is its awareness of itself.

As it comes to know itself more deeply, its experience expands.

That's what I mean when I say that an ordinary human becomes Narayana.

At one and the same time, I am a cell...

A human being...

And Narayana.

The only difference is how much of myself I am able to recognize at this moment.

Advait: Then perhaps our disagreement is mostly about language.

I would say:

Consciousness never changes.

You would say:

Self-awareness changes.

But there's something that troubles me.

If awareness grows...

Where did ignorance come from in the first place?

If the original consciousness was already complete...

How was it ever forgotten?
