What is Consciousness and Is It One or Many?

A conversation between Keshav and Advait exploring whether consciousness, the soul, and God are ultimately different realities—or different ways of describing the same truth.

Characters: Keshav • Advait

Keshav: Yaar, tell me something.

The soul, consciousness, God, the mind... are these all different things, or are they just different names for the same thing?

You spend all your time reading Advaita. You probably know better than I do.

Advait: Let's keep the mind out of it for a moment.

The mind is an instrument.

The real question is about the other three.

According to Advaita, the Self and Brahman are not different.

But before we go there, we need to understand what we mean by consciousness.

Tell me first.

What do you think consciousness is?

Keshav: I'm not sure if I'm right.

But it feels to me as though consciousness exists in levels.

The Mandukya Upanishad speaks of different states—waking, dreaming, deep sleep.

But I don't think those are the only divisions.

A tiny cell seems conscious in its own way.

Cells form organs.

Organs form bodies.

Bodies form worlds.

Everything experiences consciousness at its own level.

And when consciousness comes to know itself completely...

Perhaps nothing remains apart from it.

Maybe that's what people call God.

Advait: Let me ask you something.

Are there really different levels of consciousness?

Or is consciousness one, while its expression appears different at different levels?

The second possibility makes more sense to me.

Think of sunlight.

The sun doesn't change.

But a small window lets in less light than a large one.

What changes is not the sun.

Only the degree to which it is expressed.

Keshav: No... I don't completely agree.

Or maybe I do.

I also believe the brain doesn't create consciousness.

It only limits it.

Every living being has different limits.

That's why every being experiences reality differently.

But I don't think consciousness itself ever becomes greater or smaller.

What changes is its awareness of itself.

As it comes to know itself more deeply, its experience expands.

That's what I mean when I say that an ordinary human becomes Narayana.

At one and the same time, I am a cell...

A human being...

And Narayana.

The only difference is how much of myself I am able to recognize at this moment.

Advait: Then perhaps our disagreement is mostly about language.

I would say:

Consciousness never changes.

You would say:

Self-awareness changes.

But there's something that troubles me.

If awareness grows...

Where did ignorance come from in the first place?

If the original consciousness was already complete...

How was it ever forgotten?

Keshav: I don't have any proof for it.

Only an example.

Think about what happens when we wake up in the morning.

Everything doesn't return at once.

First the dream fades away.

Then we slowly become aware of where we are.

Then our eyes open.

Then control over the body returns.

It feels as though consciousness is moving through different layers.

That's why I think we're always conscious.

We're just not experiencing the same layer all the time.

Advait: That's a beautiful example.

But I see it a little differently.

I don't think consciousness itself is changing.

I think consciousness is gradually reconnecting with its instruments—the mind, the senses, and the body.

Still, your example raises another question.

If the mind is inactive and the senses are silent during deep sleep...

Then how do we wake up and say,

"I slept deeply last night."

Who knew that?

Keshav: Maybe consciousness itself did.

The dream was its experience.

The waking state is its experience.

Only the perspective changes.

And if we could somehow move beyond our identification with the body...

Perhaps we'd become aware of much more than we are now.

Maybe we're already doing far more than we realize.

We simply don't know that we're doing it.

Advait: So you're saying the limitation isn't one of ability.

It's one of identity.

We're only as limited as we believe ourselves to be.

Keshav: Maybe.

And there's another reason I think this way.

Since childhood, I've heard about the three forms of Vishnu.

One who resides in every particle.

One who pervades the entire universe.

And Mahavishnu, from whose breath countless universes emerge and dissolve.

I've always felt that these can't really be three different Vishnus.

Maybe they're three ways of looking at the same reality.

Advait: That's interesting.

I've never thought about it quite that way.

If we read those descriptions symbolically rather than literally...

It could make sense.

Perhaps they're not describing different sizes.

Perhaps they're describing different perspectives.

The same reality—

present in the smallest particle,

present in every living being,

present as the totality of existence.

Your idea reminds me of Indra's Net.

Every jewel reflects the entire net.

And the entire net is reflected in every jewel.

Perhaps that's why many traditions describe the Divine as being present both within the atom and within the cosmos.

But let me ask you something.

If that one reality is already present everywhere...

Then what exactly is liberation?
