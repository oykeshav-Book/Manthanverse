The Story of Shikhandi

A timeless story from the Mahabharat

Among the most mysterious and extraordinary characters of the Mahabharata, Shikhandi occupies a unique place.

Shikhandi was not only a great warrior but also a person whose entire life was shaped by an unfinished vow, profound humiliation, and an unwavering quest for justice.

Yet the story of Shikhandi did not begin with their birth.

It began in a previous life.

In that earlier birth, Shikhandi was born as Princess Amba, the eldest daughter of the King of Kashi. She had two younger sisters, Ambika and Ambalika.

When the time came for their marriage, a grand Swayamvara was arranged, attended by kings and princes from many kingdoms.

However, the prince of Hastinapura, Vichitravirya, was not invited.

When Bhishma learned of this, he followed the ancient Kshatriya tradition.

Single-handedly defeating every king present, he placed the three princesses upon his chariot and carried them to Hastinapura so that they could be married to his younger brother, Vichitravirya.

After arriving in Hastinapura, Amba spoke respectfully to Bhishma.

"My heart already belongs to King Shalva. At the Swayamvara, I had intended to choose him as my husband."

Hearing this, Bhishma honored her wishes.

Without hesitation, he respectfully sent Amba to King Shalva.

But an even greater injustice awaited her.

King Shalva refused to accept her.

"Bhishma defeated me in battle and carried you away. I can no longer accept you as my queen."

Amba pleaded with him.

She explained that Bhishma had never touched her and had sent her back with complete honor.

But Shalva refused to listen.

Heartbroken, Amba returned once again to Hastinapura.

She then appealed to Bhishma.

"Now that Shalva has rejected me, you must marry me. It is because of you that I have been left without a place in this world."

Bhishma, however, remembered the terrible vow he had taken long ago.

Having pledged lifelong celibacy, he firmly refused.

Within a single lifetime, Amba had now been rejected twice.

She could neither marry the man she loved nor find acceptance in Hastinapura.

Humiliated, abandoned, and filled with unbearable sorrow, she made a solemn vow.

One day, she would become the cause of Bhishma's downfall.

She sought help from many kings and urged them to challenge Bhishma in battle.

Yet none dared oppose the mighty son of Ganga.

Finally, Amba approached Lord Parashurama, the great sage and the very teacher of Bhishma himself.

She told him the entire story and begged for justice.

Parashurama ordered Bhishma to either marry Amba or fight him in battle.

Bhishma once again chose to uphold his sacred vow and refused to marry.

A terrible battle then erupted between master and disciple.

For many days they fought with unmatched skill.

Neither warrior could defeat the other.

At last, the gods and great sages intervened, bringing the battle to an end.

Yet Amba still received no justice.

Having lost all hope in the world, she turned instead to Lord Shiva.

For many years she performed severe austerities.

Pleased by her unwavering penance, Lord Shiva appeared before her.

Amba asked for only one boon.

"I wish to become the cause of Bhishma's death."

Lord Shiva replied,

"It cannot happen in this lifetime. But in your next birth, you shall indeed become the cause of Bhishma's fall."

Having received this divine promise, Amba realized that the purpose of her present life had come to an end.

She entered the sacred fire and gave up her mortal body.

But her vow remained unfulfilled.

That burning desire for justice would accompany her into another birth.

In time, she would be reborn as Shikhandini, the child of King Drupada of Panchala.

The destiny foretold by Lord Shiva had begun to unfold.

As foretold by Lord Shiva, Amba was reborn in her next life as the child of King Drupada of Panchala.

According to several traditions, King Drupada himself had performed severe penance to Lord Shiva.

In return, Shiva granted him a remarkable boon.

"A child shall be born to you who will one day become the cause of Bhishma's downfall."

When the destined time arrived, the queen gave birth to a daughter.

Remembering Shiva's prophecy, King Drupada believed that this child was destined to fulfill that divine purpose.

To protect the secret, he ordered that no one should know the truth of the child's birth.

Throughout the kingdom it was announced that a son had been born.

The child was named Shikhandini, yet was raised as a prince from the very beginning.

Growing up within the royal palace, Shikhandini mastered archery, swordsmanship, chariot warfare, and every branch of military science.

Only a handful of trusted people knew that Shikhandini had actually been born female.

Years passed.

When Shikhandini reached adulthood, King Drupada arranged a marriage with the daughter of King Hiranyavarma of Dasharna.

Soon after the wedding, however, the truth was discovered.

King Hiranyavarma was furious.

He believed that the King of Panchala had deceived his family.

He sent a stern message to Drupada, declaring that unless this insult was answered, he would invade Panchala.

King Drupada found himself trapped in a grave crisis.

Shikhandini, too, was overwhelmed with sorrow.

Believing that she had become the cause of her father's humiliation and the possible destruction of his kingdom, she quietly left the palace and wandered alone into the forest.

There she encountered a compassionate Yaksha named Sthunakarna.

The Yaksha gently asked the reason for her grief.

Shikhandini revealed everything—her previous birth as Amba, Lord Shiva's prophecy, the secret of her upbringing, and the danger now facing her father.

Deeply moved by her suffering, Sthunakarna made an extraordinary offer.

"For a time, I shall give you my manhood and take your womanhood upon myself. When your troubles have ended, we shall exchange our forms once again."

By the divine power of the Yaksha, their genders were exchanged.

Shikhandini now truly became a man.

He returned to Panchala.

When King Hiranyavarma investigated the matter, he became convinced that Shikhandi was indeed male.

His anger subsided, and the threatened war between the two kingdoms was peacefully avoided.

Yet another remarkable event soon followed.

The lord of the Yakshas, Kubera, arrived and discovered what Sthunakarna had done.

Displeased by this exchange, Kubera pronounced a curse.

"You shall remain in female form until Shikhandi's life comes to an end."

Later, at the request of the other Yakshas, Kubera softened the curse.

Even so, it was decreed that Shikhandi would remain male for the rest of his life.

From that day onward, he was recognized as one of the great warriors of Panchala and eventually became one of its foremost Maharathis.

Yet the true purpose of his life still remained unfulfilled.

Deep within him still burned the memory of Amba's humiliation and the promise given long ago by Lord Shiva.

The great war of Kurukshetra was drawing near.

The moment destined by fate—the confrontation between Shikhandi and Bhishma—was finally approaching.

Time passed, and at last the long-awaited day arrived.

The great war of Kurukshetra began between the Kauravas and the Pandavas.

The first commander of the Kaurava army was Bhishma Pitamaha, revered as one of the greatest archers and most formidable warriors of his age.

He possessed the divine boon of Iccha Mrityu—the power to choose the moment of his own death.

For the first nine days of the war, Bhishma devastated the Pandava army.

No warrior could withstand him for long.

Even Arjuna, despite his unmatched skill, could not fight with his full strength because of the deep love and respect he held for his grandfather.

Day after day, the Pandava forces suffered heavy losses.

On the night before the tenth day, the Pandavas, accompanied by Lord Krishna, approached Bhishma himself.

With great humility they asked,

"Grandfather, how can you be defeated?"

Bhishma answered without hesitation.

"So long as I hold my weapons, no one can defeat me. But I shall never raise a weapon against a woman, nor against one who was a woman in a previous birth."

Bhishma knew the truth of Shikhandi's previous life.

He knew that Shikhandi was none other than Amba, whose life had been filled with sorrow because of circumstances in which he himself had played a part.

The following morning, Lord Krishna turned to Arjuna and said,

"Partha, place Shikhandi before you today. Bhishma will not fight against him. The destined moment of his fall has arrived."

The battle began.

Shikhandi rode directly toward Bhishma.

The moment Bhishma saw him, he lowered his bow.

He refused to release even a single arrow.

Standing behind Shikhandi, Arjuna drew the Gandiva and unleashed a relentless shower of arrows.

Bhishma knew he possessed the strength to resist them.

Yet he chose not to defend himself.

With complete serenity, he accepted every arrow that struck his body.

Within moments, so many arrows had pierced him that he could no longer remain standing upon his chariot.

At last, he fell.

Yet his body never touched the earth.

The countless arrows fired by Arjuna held him aloft, forming the famous Bed of Arrows (Sharashayya).

A profound silence spread across the battlefield.

Warriors from both armies gathered around the fallen grandsire.

Lord Krishna also stood beside him.

Bhishma then said to Arjuna,

"Partha, my head hangs low. Give me a pillow worthy of a Kshatriya."

Understanding his grandfather's wish, Arjuna shot three arrows into the ground with perfect precision.

Bhishma gently rested his head upon them.

After some time, Bhishma spoke again.

"I am thirsty."

Arjuna drew another arrow and shot it deep into the earth.

Immediately, a pure stream of water burst forth from the ground and flowed directly to Bhishma's lips.

Greatly pleased, Bhishma blessed Arjuna with heartfelt affection.

Thus the boon granted long ago by Lord Shiva was finally fulfilled.

Amba, reborn as Shikhandi, had indeed become the cause of Bhishma's fall.

The Mahabharata, however, makes an important distinction.

Bhishma was not slain by Shikhandi's arrows.

He fell to the arrows of Arjuna.

Shikhandi became the instrument through which Lord Shiva's prophecy and destiny were fulfilled.

Even then, Bhishma did not immediately leave his mortal body.

Because of the boon of Iccha Mrityu, he remained upon the Bed of Arrows until the sun began its northward journey during Uttarayana.

There, from his bed of arrows, began the final and perhaps greatest chapter of his life, as he instructed Yudhishthira in the principles of Dharma, kingship, righteousness, and liberation.

Even after Bhishma lay upon his Bed of Arrows, the great war of Kurukshetra did not come to an end.

The battle continued for many more days, and Shikhandi remained one of the foremost warriors fighting on the side of the Pandavas.

Although the greatest purpose of his life—the fulfillment of Lord Shiva's prophecy and the downfall of Bhishma—had already been accomplished, Shikhandi did not leave the battlefield.

He continued to fight until the very end in the cause of Dharma.

After Bhishma's fall, the command of the Kaurava army successively passed to Dronacharya, Karna, and finally Shalya.

With each passing day, the war grew even more devastating.

By the eighteenth day, the mighty Kaurava army had been almost entirely destroyed.

Yet one final tragedy still remained.

During the night, while the victorious Pandava camp rested peacefully, Ashwatthama, accompanied by Kripacharya and Kritavarma, launched a sudden attack.

Consumed by grief and burning with the desire to avenge the death of his father, Dronacharya, Ashwatthama entered the sleeping camp.

He mercilessly slew many of the remaining warriors while they lay defenseless.

According to the Mahabharata, Shikhandi was among those who were killed that very night.

Thus ended the life of the warrior who had been born to fulfill a single destiny—the fall of Bhishma.

Shikhandi's life remains one of the most extraordinary stories in the Mahabharata.

In a previous birth, he had lived as Princess Amba, who suffered profound injustice.

Seeking justice, she performed severe austerities, obtained a boon from Lord Shiva, and was reborn to fulfill the vow she could not accomplish in her earlier life.

His story also reveals that the characters of the Mahabharata cannot be divided simply into heroes and villains.

Bhishma faithfully upheld his sacred vow throughout his life.

Yet that very vow unintentionally became the cause of Amba's lifelong suffering.

Likewise, Amba's pursuit of justice eventually became inseparable from a life driven by vengeance.

The Mahabharata does not present revenge as the ultimate solution.

Rather, it shows that the consequences of injustice, difficult choices, and Adharma may continue across generations.

Shikhandi is therefore remembered not merely as the instrument of Bhishma's fall, but as a powerful symbol of karma, destiny, justice, vows, and the mysterious unfolding of time.

His life reminds us that every action carries consequences, and that when the proper time arrives, justice inevitably reveals itself through the course of destiny.

Sources

Mahabharata (primarily Adi Parva, Udyoga Parva, Bhishma Parva, Drona Parva, and Sauptika Parva).
