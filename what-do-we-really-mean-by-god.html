What Do We Really Mean by "God"?

A thoughtful conversation between Advait and Nakul about one of humanity's oldest questions. Is God a person, a force, nature, consciousness, or simply another name for ultimate reality?

Characters: Advait • Nakul

Nakul

Do you really believe in God?

Advait

Yes, and no.

Nakul

What kind of answer is that? Either you do or you don't.

Advait

Both. I have no doubt that God exists, but I don't believe God is what people generally think He is, or that He is confined within the boundaries people create.

Nakul

If God isn't what people think He is, then how do you know He exists at all?

Advait

Let me ask you something first. When you say you don't believe in God, what exactly is it that you don't believe exists?

Nakul

I don't believe there's a separate conscious being who created the universe, watches everything, and makes decisions.

Advait

Don't you think humans themselves do all those things?

Nakul

We build houses, create computers, have children. But that doesn't mean the entire reality was created by some consciousness. We are still operating within the laws of nature.

Advait

You're a science student, right? Tell me, is a cell living or non-living?

Nakul

A cell is living.

Advait

And what's the hierarchy from a cell to a human?

Nakul

Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Human Organism.

Advait

Are organs living?

Nakul

Yes.

Advait

So if cells are living, tissues made of cells are living, organs made of tissues are living, organ systems are living, and finally a human is living... then at the microscopic level, aren't we a higher living being for those cells? Wouldn't we be their "God"?

Nakul

I'd say we're a higher-level system. Whether that makes us "God" is another question.

Advait

Fine. Then tell me, what makes a cell alive? Cells are made of DNA, RNA, proteins and other molecules. What makes them function?

Nakul

Science says DNA and RNA are not living. They're molecules. They function because of their chemical properties and the laws of nature.

Advait

Then who made those laws? If there's no higher reality, who created the laws of nature? And if you treat nature as the ultimate source, aren't you simply calling nature "God"?

Nakul

Fair point. If I accept nature as the ultimate reality, then yes, I've accepted an ultimate principle. My objection is only to the idea of God as a supernatural person watching over everything.

Advait

Let me ask you something else.

If cells combine to form tissues, tissues form organs, organs form organ systems, and together they form a human being, then is it possible that we, collectively, are forming a higher being whose level of consciousness is beyond ours? And perhaps that being is part of an even higher being, continuing infinitely. Likewise, below the cell there may be smaller and smaller active levels extending infinitely.

Nakul

It's an interesting hypothesis. Science wouldn't call it impossible, but there's no evidence for it either.

Advait

There are references in our traditions. We worship the Earth as Mother, rivers as goddesses, mountains as deities, planets as devas, and stars and constellations as conscious entities. Science also studies the Earth, the Solar System, galaxies, and the universe as distinct systems—it simply doesn't call them living.

Nakul

I understand. But a system and a living being are not necessarily the same thing.

Advait

That's not my point. My point is this: Are we a living being from the perspective of a cell? Does a cell know our level of consciousness? Does its belief or disbelief affect our existence? Do we love or hate any particular cell?

Nakul

No. A cell probably has no idea that a human exists. Whether it believes in us or not changes nothing. And I don't love or hate individual cells; they're simply parts of one body.

Advait

Exactly. So whether we believe in God or not may make no difference to God's existence. Our belief cannot create or destroy reality.

Nakul

I agree with that. If a higher reality exists, our belief doesn't affect it. But that still doesn't prove it exists.

Advait

I never said I wanted to prove God. That's why I said in the beginning, "I believe in God, and I don't."

Whenever you say, "I don't believe in God," my answer is: Why does it need to be proved? What changes by believing or not believing?

Nakul

So what exactly do you mean when you say, "I believe in God, and I don't"?

Advait

If God exists, then I'm not separate from Him. I'm a part of that reality.

And if God doesn't exist, then whatever exists—nature, existence, reality—is all there is. In that case too, everything is unfolding through the same reality. So practically, very little changes.

The reason I still say "God exists" is because the moment you accept nature as the ultimate reality, you've already accepted that there is something beyond your complete knowledge.

Nakul

So you're saying I'm rejecting one definition of God while unknowingly accepting another?

Advait

Exactly.

Nakul

Then what you're really saying is that reality doesn't depend on belief.

Advait

Exactly.

Advait

You rejected the idea of God as a supernatural vigilante sitting somewhere above the universe.

But if you accept an ultimate reality that gives rise to natural laws and sustains existence, you've only changed the name. You call it "Nature"; I call it "God."

Nakul

Then our disagreement isn't really about whether something ultimate exists.

It's about what we choose to call it, and whether that ultimate reality is conscious.

Nakul

But let me say one thing.

I don't think I'll say so easily anymore that "God doesn't exist."

I'd rather say:

"I don't know what the ultimate reality is."

And honestly, that seems like a more scientifically honest answer.

There's still one place where we differ.

I wouldn't automatically equate "ultimate reality" with "God" unless you first explain exactly what you mean by the word God.

Because if your idea of God isn't an old man with a beard sitting somewhere in the sky, but rather the ultimate reality from which everything emerges, then our disagreement is no longer about existence—it's mostly about definition.

😄 And honestly... I think you've taken me beyond the usual atheist vs. theist debate.

This has become a much deeper philosophical conversation.

Advait

That's all I wanted.

I'm not trying to make you religious.

I'm only trying to make sure that neither of us mistakes a definition for the truth.

Maybe we're looking at the same reality through different words.

And perhaps the search matters more than the label.
